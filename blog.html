Before the world wakes up — my favourite times of day

There are two moments in every day that belong only to me. Before Fajr, and at midnight. Everything else is shared — these hours are mine.

Before Fajr — the sacred silence

There is something about the hour before the sun rises that no other part of the day can replicate. The world is completely still. No notifications, no noise, no expectations from anyone. Just you, your intentions, and whatever work you decide to do with that quiet.

I pray Fajr and then I sit down and work. And I mean really work — with a level of focus that simply isn't available to me at any other point in the day. The mind is fresh, the soul is settled after prayer, and the absence of distraction makes everything sharper.

There is also something deeply motivating about knowing that most people are still asleep. Not in a competitive way — but in the sense that you are building something in the quiet that others will only see later. The results of a pre-dawn hour compound over time in a way that is hard to explain until you experience it.

Midnight — when the world gives up

My second favourite time is midnight. By that point most people have stopped. They've watched their shows, scrolled their phones, and gone to bed. The world has essentially decided that the day is done.

But I haven't. And there is a particular energy that comes from working while everyone else is asleep — a feeling of ownership over your own hours that is deeply satisfying.

Midnight work is different from morning work. In the morning you are building for the day ahead. At midnight you are finishing what you started — or starting something that nobody knows about yet. Both are powerful in their own way.

What these hours have in common

Silence. Ownership. A sense that the time belongs entirely to you. These are the hours I protect most fiercely in my week — because they are the ones where I feel most like myself and most capable of doing the things that actually matter to me.

If you haven't tried working in either of these windows — try it once. Just once. You might never go back.

My morning routine — how I build the day before the day builds me

A routine isn't a restriction — it's a foundation. Mine took years to shape. Here is what it looks like now.

Step 1 — Cardio

Before anything else, I move. Some form of cardio — whether that's a run, a session on the bike, or something else depending on how the body feels. The purpose isn't just physical. Moving first thing resets the nervous system, clears whatever mental fog came with waking up, and creates a baseline of energy that carries through the rest of the morning.

I don't check my phone before this. I don't read the news. I don't open any apps. The first act of my day is deliberate physical movement — not consumption.

Step 2 — Eat

After cardio comes food. Not coffee first — food first. The body has been fasting through the night and through the workout. Fuelling it properly before anything else is non-negotiable.

Step 3 — Coffee and reading

This is the part of the morning I look forward to most. Coffee in hand, book open. No screens, no social media — just a book and a hot drink.

Reading in the morning does something specific to the way I think for the rest of the day. It puts language and ideas in my head early — and those ideas tend to resurface throughout the day in useful ways. It is one of the best investments I make in myself every morning.

Step 4 — Work with Saleh Al Durazi

Then I work. And almost always, I work while listening to Saleh Al Durazi. There is something about his recitations that creates the right internal environment for deep, focused work. It isn't background noise — it is a presence that keeps the mind grounded while the hands are busy.

By the time most people are starting their day, I've already prayed, moved, eaten, read and worked. That isn't productivity — it's identity. This is who I am before the world asks anything of me.

Why I love summer — and what it does to me

Summer in Bahrain is not gentle. It is heavy, relentless, and completely alive. And I love it for every one of those reasons.

The heat is honest

There is nothing soft about a Bahraini summer. The sun doesn't ease you in — it hits you the moment you step outside and reminds you immediately of where you are. I find that honesty refreshing. The season makes no apologies and demands nothing from you except that you show up and deal with it.

That directness resonates with me. I've always respected things that don't pretend to be something they aren't — and summer never pretends.

The energy is different

Something about the intensity of summer shifts the energy around it. People who love comfort avoid it — which means the spaces, the early mornings by the water, the late nights on rooftops — feel different. More alive in a way that is hard to explain to someone who sees heat as only an obstacle.

It matches something inside me

I am not a slow-burn kind of person. I prefer intensity. I prefer pressure. I prefer the feeling of being fully in something rather than half in it. Summer is the only season that operates at the same frequency.

Spring is beautiful but too transitional. Autumn feels like retreat. Winter in Bahrain is mild — pleasant but forgettable. Summer is the only season that demands your full attention. And I respect demands.
